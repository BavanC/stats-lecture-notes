Understanding Multiple Predictors and Interaction Effects

Multiple Regression

Multiple regression is an extension of simple regression. Instead of using just one predictor variable, we use two or more to try and predict an outcome. The goal is to see if we can create a better predictive model by including more relevant information.

The Rationale: Explaining More Variance

Each predictor variable explains a certain amount of variance in the outcome. By combining predictors, we hope to explain more of the total variance than any single predictor could alone. The model tests the combined predictive power of all your chosen variables.

A Worked Example: What Predicts Statistics Exam Scores?

Let's try to predict students' statistics exam scores (our outcome). We'll use three predictor variables: Fear of Stats, Fear of Computers, and Fear of Maths.

Application Corner: Comparing Predictors

When you have multiple predictors, you might want to know which one is the most important or strongest. You cannot use the 'Unstandardized B' for this, as the predictors might be on different scales.

Instead, you must look at the Standardized Beta (β) column. This puts all predictors on the same scale, so they can be directly compared.

Example: In our Coefficients table, 'Stats Fear' has a β of 0.529, while 'Computer Fear' has a β of -0.086 and 'Maths Fear' has a β of 0.070. By comparing the absolute values, we can see that 0.529 is much larger than the others, making 'Stats Fear' the strongest predictor in the model.

The Regression Equation

The 'Unstandardized' coefficients from the table allow us to build the regression equation. The general form is: Y' = b_0 + b_1X_1 + b_2X_2 + ... + b_kX_k.

Exam Score' = -6.315 + (2.384 * Stats Fear) + (-0.482 * Computer Fear) + (0.662 * Maths Fear)

Using this formula, you could plug in a student's scores on the three "fear" scales to get a predicted exam score.

Assumptions of Multiple Regression

For the results of a multiple regression to be reliable and valid, several assumptions must be met. Some are considered during the study design, while others are checked after the analysis.

Key Assumptions to Check
  • Linearity: The relationship between each predictor and the outcome should be linear. This can be initially checked by looking at scatterplots between each predictor and the outcome.
  • No Multicollinearity: Your predictors should not be too highly correlated with each other. High correlation makes it difficult for the model to determine the unique contribution of each predictor.
    • Check: Look at a correlation matrix of the predictors. Correlations above r = .80 are a concern.
    • Check: Use 'Collinearity Diagnostics'. The Tolerance value should not be less than 0.2, and the VIF (Variance Inflation Factor) should not be greater than 10.
  • Homoscedasticity (Homogeneity of Variance): The variance of the errors (residuals) should be consistent across all levels of the predicted values.
    • Check: Look at a scatterplot of the Standardized Residuals vs. the Predicted Values. You want to see a random cloud of dots with no obvious pattern (like a funnel or cone shape). A pattern suggests heteroscedasticity, which is a violation of the assumption.
  • Normality of Residuals: The errors your model makes should be approximately normally distributed.
    • Check: Look at a Q-Q plot of the standardized residuals. The points should fall closely along the diagonal line.
    • Check: A histogram of the standardized residuals should look roughly like a normal bell curve.

What is Moderation?

In regression, we often want to know more than just whether one variable predicts another. We want to know under what conditions that prediction holds true. This is where moderation comes in.

A moderator is a variable that changes the strength or direction of the relationship between a predictor (IV) and an outcome (DV).

Essentially, a moderator analysis tests for an interaction. It tells you that the effect of your main predictor isn't constant‚Äîit depends on the level of your moderator variable.

Example 1: Experience as a Moderator of Memory

This classic example explores whether expertise (years of experience) moderates the effect of list structure on memory recall. Researchers compared expert taxi drivers and novices on their ability to recall a configured route versus a random list of streets.

Visualizing the Interaction

The bar chart clearly shows the interaction. For Novice Students, performance is nearly identical for both list types. For Expert Taxi Drivers, however, recall is vastly superior for the real-world route compared to the random list. This demonstrates that expertise moderates the effect of list structure on memory.
