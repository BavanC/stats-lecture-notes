Core Statistical Tests in Psychology

A Practical Guide to Understanding and Reporting Key Analyses

The Foundations of Testing

What is 'Statistical Significance'?

At its heart, statistics is about one thing: figuring out if an effect we see in our data is a real effect or if it could have just happened by random chance.

The P-Value: The probability of getting your results purely by chance.

The Rule (The 'Line in the Sand'): In psychology, we generally agree that if the probability of getting a result by chance is less than 5%, we call it "statistically significant." This is why we're always looking for p < .05$.

The Core Logic: Hypothesis Testing & The Risks of Being Wrong

Every statistical test is a "courtroom" drama where we put the "no effect" idea on trial.

  • Null Hypothesis ($H_0$): The "skeptic's view." It assumes there is no effect.
  • Alternative Hypothesis ($H_1$): Your research hypothesis. It states that there is an effect.
  • The Goal: We use the p-value to decide if we have enough evidence to reject the null hypothesis.

But what if we make the wrong decision? There are two ways we can be wrong:

  • Type I Error ($\alpha$): A "False Positive". This is when we reject the null hypothesis when it was actually true.
  • Type II Error ($\beta$): A "Miss". This is when we fail to reject the null hypothesis when it was actually false.

Standardization & Z-Scores: The Great Equalizer

Often in psychology, we want to compare things that are measured on completely different scales (like exam scores out of 50 vs. anxiety ratings out of 10). A Z-score solves this problem by converting any raw score into a universal unit: the number of standard deviations it is from its own mean.

$$Z = \frac{\text{Your Score} - \text{Mean of Scores}}{\text{Standard Deviation of Scores}}$$

A positive Z-score means your score was above the mean, a negative Z-score means it was below, and a Z-score of 0 means it was exactly average.

Worked Example: Who Did Better?

Amy and Ben are in different classes.

  • Amy scored 80 on her test. Her class average (mean) was 70, and the standard deviation (SD) was 10.
  • Ben scored 85 on his test. His class average (mean) was 82, and the standard deviation (SD) was 3.

Amy's Z-Score: (80 - 70) / 10 = +1.0 (Amy was exactly 1 standard deviation above her class average).

Ben's Z-Score: (85 - 82) / 3 = +1.0 (Ben was also exactly 1 standard deviation above his class average).

Conclusion: Even though their raw scores were different, they performed equally well relative to their respective classmates. Standardization allows for a fair comparison.

Application Corner: The Secret to Correlation

How can you correlate two things measured on different scales, like height (in cm) and weight (in kg)? The answer is Z-scores!

Before correlating, the analysis engine first converts all height scores into Z-scores and all weight scores into Z-scores. Now both variables are on the same standardized scale. The correlation is then calculated based on these Z-scores.

Understanding Spread: Variance and Standard Deviation

While the mean tells us the central point of our data, the variance and standard deviation tell us how spread out the scores are around that mean. They are crucial for understanding the 'Noise' part of our statistical tests.

Variance

Variance is the average of the squared differences from the mean. It gives us a measure of how much the data varies.

We square the differences for a simple reason: to stop positive and negative differences from cancelling each other out. The formula is:

$$s^2 = \frac{\sum(x_i - \bar{x})^2}{N-1}$$

Standard Deviation (SD)

The standard deviation is simply the square root of the variance. This is incredibly useful because it returns the measure of spread to the original units of our data, making it much easier to interpret.

If the mean exam score is 65 and the SD is 5, it gives us a much clearer idea of the score distribution than knowing the variance is 25.

$$s = \sqrt{\frac{\sum(x_i - \bar{x})^2}{N-1}}$$

Mathematical Notation Cheat Sheet

Breaking Down the Formulas

The formulas for variance and standard deviation can look intimidating, but each symbol has a straightforward meaning:

  • $s^2$: This is the symbol for sample variance. The 's' stands for sample, and the '2' indicates that the value is squared.
  • $s$: This is the symbol for sample standard deviation. It's just the square root of the variance.
  • $\sum$ (Sigma): This is the summation symbol. It's a mathematical instruction that simply means "add everything up".
  • $x_i$: This represents each individual score in your dataset.
  • $\bar{x}$ (x-bar): This is the symbol for the mean (or average) of all the scores in your sample.
  • $N$: This represents the total number of scores in your sample.

So, the expression $\sum(x_i - \bar{x})^2$ is just a set of instructions: "For each individual score, subtract the mean, square the result, and then add all of those squared results together."

The t-Test

Use a t-test when your research question is about the difference in mean scores between exactly two groups.

The Core Logic: Signal vs. Noise

Think of a t-test as a simple fraction:

$$t = \frac{\text{Difference between group means (Signal)}}{\text{Variability within the groups (Noise)}}$$

What are Degrees of Freedom (df)?

Degrees of freedom is a tricky concept, but you can think of it as a measure of how much trustworthy information your data provides. It's closely related to your sample size. The more degrees of freedom you have, the more confident you can be that your sample is representative of the whole population. For different tests, the df is calculated slightly differently, but your stats software will always give you the correct value to report.

Choosing the Right t-Test

The key is your experimental design. Ask yourself this one question: "How many scores did each participant provide for this comparison?"

Paired-Samples t-Test

Use this if each participant provided two scores (one for each condition).

  • AKA: Dependent t-Test, Within-Subjects t-Test.
  • Example (Before & After): Testing the same people's stress levels before and after a meditation app.

Independent-Samples t-Test

Use this if each participant provided only one score.

  • AKA: Between-Subjects t-Test.
  • Example (Treatment vs. Control): One group gets a new drug, a separate group gets a placebo. You compare the outcomes.

How to Report a t-Test

The format is: t(df) = t-value, p = p-value.

Example Write-Up:

"A paired-samples t-test showed a significant difference between the 'before' scores (M = 13.4, SD = 3.7) and the 'after' scores (M = 11.5, SD = 2.9), t(19) = 3.472, p = .003."

Beyond Significance: Effect Size

Why P-Values Aren't Enough

Getting a "significant" p-value (p < .05) is exciting. It tells us that our result is probably not due to random chance. But it doesn't tell us the whole story. A p-value can be significant even for a tiny, unimportant effect if you have a very large sample size.

This is where effect size comes in. It answers a more practical question.

The P-Value asks: "Is there a real effect?"

Effect Size asks: "Okay, but how BIG is the effect?"

What is Effect Size?

Simply put, the effect size tells you how much of an impact your independent variable had on your dependent variable. It's a measure of the magnitude, or strength, of a phenomenon.

One of the most common effect sizes for comparing two means (like in a t-test) is Cohen's d. It measures the difference between two means in standard deviation units. This is useful because it gives us a "standardized" value that we can compare across different studies, even if they used different scales of measurement.

Interpreting Effect Size: Cohen's d

So you have a Cohen's d value from your JASP output. What does it mean? Jacob Cohen provided some handy rules of thumb that are widely used in psychology:

  • d ≈ 0.2 is considered a 'small' effect.
  • d ≈ 0.5 is considered a 'medium' effect.
  • d ≈ 0.8 is considered a 'large' effect.
Visualizing Effect Size: The Overlap

A great way to understand effect size is to think about how much the scores of your two groups overlap.

  • A small effect size (d=0.2) means the two groups are very similar and their distributions overlap a lot. It's hard to tell them apart just by looking at a random score.
  • A medium effect size (d=0.5) means there is a noticeable difference, and the overlap is smaller. You can start to see a clear separation.
  • A large effect size (d=0.8) means there's a big difference between the two groups. Their distributions overlap very little, and the separation is obvious.

Knowing the effect size gives you a much better picture of the real-world importance of your finding than a p-value alone.

How to Report Effect Size

When you report your t-test results, you should always include the effect size. It gives your reader a complete picture.

Example Write-Up:

"A paired-samples t-test revealed that the difference was not significant, t(7) = -1.984, p = .088. However, the effect size was medium-to-large, d = -0.70, suggesting a potentially meaningful difference that may have been missed due to a small sample size."

One-Way Independent ANOVA

Use a One-Way Independent ANOVA (Analysis of Variance) when you have one Independent Variable with three or more levels, and each level contains different participants (between-subjects design).

The Core Idea: Analyzing Variance

In any experiment, the scores you measure will vary. ANOVA's main purpose is to figure out why they vary. It splits the total variation in your data into two types:

  • Systematic Variance: This is the variation we can explain. It's the differences between groups caused by your manipulation. This is the 'Signal'.
  • Unsystematic Variance: This is the variation we can't explain. It's the random fluctuation in scores caused by things like individual differences. This is the 'Noise'.

ANOVA's Goal: To compare the size of the systematic variance to the size of the unsystematic variance. If the 'Signal' is much bigger than the 'Noise', we can conclude that our manipulation had a real effect.

Key Assumption: Homogeneity of Variance
  • What it is: An assumption for Independent ANOVAs. It means the variance (or spread of scores) in each of your groups should be approximately the same.
  • How to check it: Use Levene's Test. You can ask for this in JASP under 'Assumption Checks'.
  • The Rule: If Levene's test is not significant (p > .05), the assumption is met and you can proceed with the standard ANOVA. If it is significant (p < .05), the assumption is violated, and you should report the result of a more robust test like the Welch's F-test.

Step 1: Calculate the Sum of Squares (SS)

First, we calculate our two core components of variance by summing up the squared distances.

Within-Groups SS ($SS_W$): The 'Noise'

The formula is $$SS_W = \sum(x_i - \bar{x}_{\text{group}})^2$$ In words, this means for every individual score ($x_i$), we subtract the mean of its group ($\bar{x}_{\text{group}}$), square the result, and then sum ($\sum$) all of these squared differences together.

After doing this for all scores, the total is: $SS_W = 1.41$

Between-Groups SS ($SS_B$): The 'Signal'

The formula is $$SS_B = \sum n_{\text{group}}(\bar{x}_{\text{group}} - \bar{x}_{\text{grand}})^2$$ In words, this means for each group, we take the difference between the group mean and the grand mean, square it, multiply it by the number of people in that group, and finally, sum the results for all groups.

Adding these up gives the total: $SS_B = 3.36$

Step 2: Calculate the Mean Squares (MS)

Next, we find the average variance by dividing the SS values by their degrees of freedom (df).

  • $df_B = (\text{Number of groups} - 1) = 3 - 1 = 2$
  • $df_W = (\text{Total participants} - \text{Number of groups}) = 18 - 3 = 15$

$$MS_B = \frac{SS_B}{df_B} = \frac{3.36}{2} = 1.68$$

$$MS_W = \frac{SS_W}{df_W} = \frac{1.41}{15} = 0.094$$

Step 3: Calculate the F-Ratio

Finally, we divide the 'Signal' by the 'Noise' to get our F-ratio.

$$F = \frac{MS_B}{MS_W} = \frac{1.68}{0.094} = 17.87$$

Application Corner: Calculating Standard Error

Error bars on graphs often show the Standard Error of the Mean (SEM), which tells us how precise our estimate of the group mean is. You can calculate it from the descriptive statistics table.

Formula: SEM = Standard Deviation (SD) / √(Sample Size (N))

Example: Let's say a 'No-Caffeine' group (N=25) has a mean score of 50 and a Standard Deviation of 10.

  • SEM = 10 / √(25)
  • SEM = 10 / 5
  • SEM = 2.0

So, the error bar for this group would extend from 48 to 52 (50 ± 2.0).

One-Way Repeated-Measures ANOVA

Use a One-Way Repeated-Measures ANOVA when you have one Independent Variable with three or more levels, and the same participants are tested in all conditions (within-subjects design).

The Big Advantage: More Power

Repeated-measures designs are powerful. Because the same people are in each condition, we can account for the 'noise' caused by individual differences. By mathematically removing this individual 'noise', we make our error term smaller.

A smaller denominator (Noise) leads to a larger F-ratio, making the test more sensitive to finding a real effect.

$$F = \frac{\text{Signal}}{\text{Noise} - \text{Individual Differences}}$$

Key Assumption: Sphericity
  • What it is: An assumption for Repeated-Measures ANOVAs with 3 or more levels. It means the variances of the differences between all possible pairs of conditions are roughly equal.
  • How to check it: Use Mauchly's Test of Sphericity.
  • The Rule: If Mauchly's test is not significant (p > .05), the assumption is met and you can use the standard "Sphericity Assumed" ANOVA results. If it is significant (p < .05), the assumption is violated, and you must report the results using a correction (e.g., the Greenhouse-Geisser or Huynh-Feldt correction). JASP provides these automatically.

Follow-Up Tests for ANOVA

A significant F-ratio in an ANOVA is an exciting start! It tells you that there's a difference somewhere among your groups. However, it's an omnibus test, meaning it doesn't tell you which specific groups differ from each other. To find that out, you need to conduct follow-up tests.

There are two main types, and the one you choose depends on your research plan.

Planned Comparisons (Contrasts)

You use planned comparisons when you had specific, theory-driven hypotheses before you even collected the data. You're not just exploring; you're testing pre-defined questions.

  • Example Hypotheses: "I predict that any drug (Joyzepam or Anxifree) will be better than a placebo" and "I predict Joyzepam will be more effective than Anxifree."
  • Advantage: They are more statistically powerful than post-hoc tests, meaning you have a better chance of finding a real effect if one exists.
  • How it works: You assign weights to your groups to create "chunks" of variance to compare. For example, to compare both drugs to the placebo, you could assign weights like: Placebo = -2, Anxifree = +1, Joyzepam = +1.

Example Write-Up:

"Planned contrasts revealed that participants in the drug groups had a significantly larger mood gain than those who received a placebo, t(15) = 4.27, p < .001$. A second contrast showed that participants who took Joyzepam experienced a significantly larger mood gain than those who took Anxifree, t(15) = 4.36, p < .001$."

Application Corner: Decoding Planned Contrasts

Contrast codes can look confusing, but they are just asking specific questions. The key is that positive numbers are compared against negative numbers, and groups coded '0' are ignored.

Scenario: A study compares four therapies: Control (no therapy), CBT, Psychodynamic, and Humanistic.

  • Contrast 2 is asking: "Ignoring the Control group, is the CBT group different from the average of the Psychodynamic and Humanistic groups?" (Red vs. Green, Control is ignored)

Post-Hoc Tests

You use post-hoc tests when you don't have specific hypotheses beforehand. You're in an exploratory mode, comparing every possible pair of groups to see where the differences lie.

  • Example Question: "Is there any difference between Placebo, Anxifree, and Joyzepam?"
  • Disadvantage: Because you're doing multiple tests, the risk of a Type I error (a false positive) increases. To combat this, post-hoc tests include a correction.
  • Common Corrections: The Bonferroni correction is a common but very conservative choice. It adjusts the p-value for each comparison to keep the overall error rate at .05. JASP offers several options.

Example Write-Up:

"A one-way ANOVA revealed a significant effect of drug on mood gain, F(2, 15) = 19.3, p < .001$. Post-hoc tests using the Bonferroni correction indicated that Joyzepam produced a significantly larger mood change than both Anxifree (p = .002) and the placebo (p < .001). The difference between Anxifree and the placebo was not significant (p = .451)."

Two-Way Independent ANOVA

A Two-Way ANOVA is used when you have two independent variables (also called factors). For example, a 2x2 design has two IVs, each with two levels. A 2x3 design has two IVs, one with two levels and one with three. A Two-Way ANOVA allows you to test:

  • Main Effect of IV 1: The overall effect of your first factor, ignoring the levels of the second.
  • Main Effect of IV 2: The overall effect of your second factor, ignoring the levels of the first.
  • Interaction Effect: This is the most interesting part. It tests whether the effect of one independent variable depends on the level of the other independent variable.

Application Corner: Counting the F-Ratios

A Two-Way ANOVA always tests three things, so you will always get three important F-ratios in your output table.

  • One F-ratio for the Main Effect of your first IV.
  • One F-ratio for the Main Effect of your second IV.
  • One F-ratio for the Interaction between them.

The Golden Rule of Interactions: Always interpret the interaction effect first! If it's significant, it qualifies the main effects. This means the main effects might be misleading on their own, because the interaction tells you the full story is more complex.

Two-Way Repeated-Measures ANOVA

Use this design when you have two independent variables and the same participants take part in all possible conditions.

Application Corner: Decoding the Design

When you see a design described as, for example, a "2x3 repeated-measures design", this tells you everything you need to know:

  • The number of numbers tells you how many IVs there are. Here, "2x3" has two numbers, so there are two IVs.
  • Each number tells you how many levels that IV has. So, one IV has 2 levels (e.g., Sugar: Normal, Extra) and the second IV has 3 levels (e.g., Caffeine: None, Low, High).
  • The total number of conditions is just the numbers multiplied: 2 * 3 = 6 conditions.

This indicates that a high level of caffeine increases the popularity of the drink, but only when it has a normal amount of sugar."

Mixed ANOVA

A Mixed ANOVA is your tool of choice when you have a mix of variable types: at least one between-subjects IV (different groups of people) and at least one within-subjects IV (everyone does all levels).

Application Corner: What Makes a Design "Mixed"?

A "mixed" design simply means you have a mix of IV types. To identify one, look for:

  • At least one Between-Subjects IV: Where you compare different groups of people. (Example: Comparing an 'Expert' group to a 'Novice' group).
  • At least one Within-Subjects IV: Where the same people are tested in all conditions. (Example: Testing everyone on a 'Configured' list AND a 'Random' list).

If your design has both types, it's a mixed design.

A Worked Example: Expert Memory

Researchers test memory for London street names. There are two IVs: Expertise (Expert cab drivers vs. Novice students) and List Type (Configured route vs. Random list). Expertise is a between-subjects factor (you're either an expert or a novice), but List Type is a within-subjects factor, as everyone is tested on both list types.

Correlation

While ANOVA looks for differences between group means, correlation looks for relationships between continuous variables. It tells us if two variables tend to move together, and how strongly.

Application Corner: The Heart of Correlation

How can you correlate two things measured on different scales, like height (cm) and weight (kg)?

The answer is standardization. Before correlating, the analysis converts all scores on each variable into standardized scores (also known as Z-scores). A Z-score simply expresses how many standard deviations a raw score is from its own mean. This puts both variables onto the same standardized scale.

At its core, the correlation calculation is based on the sum of the products of these standardized scores for each participant. This standardized approach is what makes correlation so versatile.

Visualizing Relationships: Scatterplots

The best way to start understanding a correlation is to look at a scatterplot. Each dot represents one participant's scores on two variables. The overall pattern of the dots gives you a visual clue about the strength and direction of the relationship.

The Correlation Coefficient (Pearson's r)

The result of a correlation is a single number, Pearson's r, which tells us two crucial things about the linear relationship between two variables:

  • Direction: Is the relationship positive or negative? A positive r means as one variable goes up, the other tends to go up. A negative r means as one goes up, the other tends to go down.
  • Strength: How strong is the relationship? The value of r ranges from -1 to +1.
    • r close to 0 = very weak or no linear relationship.
    • r close to 1 or -1 = very strong linear relationship.
    • General rules of thumb for psychology: ±.1 (small), ±.3 (medium), ±.5 (large).
The Concept of Shared Variance ($R^2$)

If you square the correlation coefficient (r), you get the coefficient of determination ($R^2$). This value tells you how much of the variance in one variable is "explained" or "shared" by the other variable.

For example, if the correlation between revision hours and exam score is r = .72, then $R^2 = .72^2 = 0.5184$.

This means that 51.84% of the differences we see in students' exam scores can be accounted for by the differences in their revision hours. The rest of the variance is due to other factors (e.g., sleep, intelligence, luck).

Crucial point: Correlation does not equal causation! Just because two variables are related, it doesn't mean one causes the other. There could be a third, unmeasured variable (a confounding variable) influencing both.

How to Report a Correlation

The format is: r(df) = r-value, p = p-value. The degrees of freedom (df) for a Pearson correlation is N-2, where N is the number of pairs.

Example Write-Up:

"A Pearson's correlation was run to determine the relationship between hours spent revising and final exam score. There was a strong, positive correlation between the two variables, r(28) = .72, p < .001$. This indicates that students who spent more time revising tended to achieve higher exam scores."

Simple Linear Regression

Regression takes correlation a step further. Instead of just describing a relationship, it allows us to predict the value of one variable (the outcome or dependent variable) from the value of another (the predictor or independent variable).

Key Tip: In a Simple Linear Regression (with only one predictor), the Standardized Beta (β) value in the 'Coefficients' table is exactly the same as the Pearson's r correlation coefficient.

The Line of Best Fit & The Regression Equation

Simple regression finds the single best straight line that goes through the data points on a scatterplot. This line minimizes the overall distance between itself and all the individual data points (this is called the method of least squares). The line is described by a simple equation:

$$Y = b_0 + b_1X$$

Regression Plot: The Line of Best Fit

This equation has two key parts that you can see on the graph:

  • $b_0$ (The Intercept): This is the value of Y when X is 0. It's the point where the red regression line crosses the vertical Y-axis. In our example, if someone did 0 hours of revision, we would predict their exam score to be the intercept value.
  • $b_1$ (The Slope): This represents the steepness of the red line. It tells us how much Y is predicted to change for every one-unit increase in X. A steeper line means a larger slope and a stronger prediction. A flat line means the predictor has no effect.
  • Y and X: Y is the outcome variable we are trying to predict (the value on the Y-axis), and X is the predictor variable we are using (the value on the X-axis).

Application Corner: Making a Prediction

You can use the regression equation from your output to predict a score. Let's use an example where we predict plant growth (cm) from hours of sunlight (Sunlight).

JASP Output gives us: Intercept ($b_0$) = 5.2, and the slope for Sunlight ($b_1$) = 2.1.

Equation: Growth = 5.2 + (2.1 * Sunlight)

Question: What is the predicted growth for a plant that gets 7 hours of sunlight?

  • Growth = 5.2 + (2.1 * 7)
  • Growth = 5.2 + 14.7
  • Predicted Growth = 19.9 cm

Multiple Regression

Multiple regression is an extension of simple regression. Instead of using just one predictor variable, we use two or more to try and predict an outcome. The goal is to see if we can create a better predictive model by including more relevant information.

The Rationale: Explaining More Variance

Each predictor variable explains a certain amount of variance in the outcome. By combining predictors, we hope to explain more of the total variance than any single predictor could alone. The model tests the combined predictive power of all your chosen variables.

A Worked Example: What Predicts Statistics Exam Scores?

Let's try to predict students' statistics exam scores (our outcome). We'll use three predictor variables: Fear of Stats, Fear of Computers, and Fear of Maths.

Application Corner: Comparing Predictors

When you have multiple predictors, you might want to know which one is the most important or strongest. You cannot use the 'Unstandardized B' for this, as the predictors might be on different scales.

Instead, you must look at the Standardized Beta (β) column. This puts all predictors on the same scale, so they can be directly compared.

Example: In our Coefficients table, 'Stats Fear' has a β of 0.529, while 'Computer Fear' has a β of -0.086 and 'Maths Fear' has a β of 0.070. By comparing the absolute values, we can see that 0.529 is much larger than the others, making 'Stats Fear' the strongest predictor in the model.

Important Assumptions in Multiple Regression

Multiple regression has several key assumptions you need to check:

  • Linearity: The relationship between each predictor and the outcome should be linear.
  • Homoscedasticity: The variance of the errors (residuals) should be consistent across all levels of the predictors. You check this with a scatterplot of residuals vs. predicted values. You want to see a random cloud of dots, not a funnel shape.
  • Normality of Residuals: The errors your model makes should be normally distributed. You can check this with a Q-Q plot or a histogram of the residuals.
  • No Multicollinearity: Your predictors should not be too highly correlated with each other (e.g., $r > .80$). High correlation makes it difficult to tell which predictor is responsible for the variance. You can check this with a correlation matrix and by looking at VIF and Tolerance statistics in JASP (VIF > 10 or Tolerance < 0.1 are signs of a problem).

Which Test Should I Use? A Flowchart

  • What is my main research question?
    • Am I looking for a relationship or association between continuous variables?
      • Use Correlation or Regression.
    • Am I looking for a difference in average scores between groups?
      • How many groups (or levels of my IV) am I comparing?
        • Exactly TWO groups.
          • Use a t-Test.
        • THREE or MORE groups.
          • Use an ANOVA.

Glossary

Alternative Hypothesis ($H_1$)

Your research hypothesis; the claim that there is an effect, a difference, or a relationship.

ANOVA (Analysis of Variance)

A statistical test used to compare the means of three or more groups.

Between-Subjects Design

An experimental design where different participants are assigned to each condition or group.

Bonferroni Correction

A method used in post-hoc tests to counteract the problem of inflated Type I error that occurs when performing multiple statistical comparisons. It divides the alpha level (e.g., .05) by the number of tests being run.

Coefficient of Determination ($R^2$)

A value in regression and correlation that represents the proportion of variance in the dependent variable that can be predicted from the independent variable(s). For example, an R² of .25 means 25% of the outcome's variability is explained by the model.

Correlation

A measure of the extent to which two continuous variables are related.

Degrees of Freedom (df)

The number of values in a final calculation that are free to vary. It's related to sample size and is used to determine the critical value for a statistical test.

Dependent Variable (DV)

The variable that is measured by the experimenter. It is the 'outcome' variable that you expect to be influenced by the independent variable.

Effect Size

A measure of the magnitude or size of a statistical effect, independent of sample size. It tells you how meaningful the effect is. Examples include Cohen's d and partial eta squared ($\eta_p^2$).

F-Ratio

The test statistic calculated in an ANOVA. It is the ratio of the variance between groups (signal) to the variance within groups (noise).

Factorial ANOVA

An ANOVA that includes two or more independent variables (factors), allowing for the testing of main effects and interaction effects.

Greenhouse-Geisser Correction

An adjustment made to the degrees of freedom in a repeated-measures ANOVA when the assumption of sphericity has been violated. It's a conservative correction.

Homogeneity of Variance

An assumption of the independent-samples t-test and ANOVA that the variance in scores is the same for each group.

Independent-Samples t-Test

A test used to compare the means of two groups when each group is made up of different participants.

Interaction Effect

In a factorial ANOVA, this occurs when the effect of one independent variable on the dependent variable depends on the level of another independent variable.

Intercept ($b_0$)

In regression, this is the predicted value of the outcome variable (Y) when the predictor variable (X) is equal to zero. It's the point where the regression line crosses the y-axis.

Independent Variable (IV)

The variable that is manipulated or changed by the experimenter to see if it has an effect on the dependent variable.

Levene's Test

A test used to check the assumption of homogeneity of variance. A non-significant result (p > .05) indicates the assumption is met.

Line of Best Fit

The line on a scatterplot that comes closest to all of the dots in the plot, used in regression to make predictions.

Main Effect

In a factorial ANOVA, this is the overall effect of one independent variable on its own, ignoring the other independent variables.

Mauchly's Test

A test used to check the assumption of sphericity in repeated-measures ANOVAs. A significant result (p < .05) indicates the assumption has been violated.

Mean ($\bar{x}$)

The arithmetic average of a set of scores, calculated by adding all scores together and dividing by the number of scores.

Mixed ANOVA

An ANOVA that combines between-subjects and within-subjects factors in the same design.

Multiple Regression

A statistical technique that uses two or more predictor variables to predict a single outcome variable.

Multicollinearity

A problem in multiple regression where two or more predictor variables are very highly correlated with each other, making it difficult to determine their individual effects.

Null Hypothesis ($H_0$)

The default assumption that there is no effect, no difference, or no relationship. The goal of a statistical test is to see if there is enough evidence to reject this.

p-value

The probability of observing your data (or more extreme data) if the null hypothesis were true. A small p-value (typically < .05) suggests your result is unlikely to be due to chance.

Paired-Samples t-Test

A test used to compare the means of two conditions when the same participants took part in both conditions.

Pearson's r

The correlation coefficient, a value between -1 and +1 that indicates the strength and direction of a linear relationship between two variables.

Planned Comparisons

Follow-up tests to an ANOVA that are used when you have specific, theory-driven hypotheses about which groups will differ.

Post-Hoc Tests

Follow-up tests to an ANOVA used to compare every group with every other group to find where significant differences lie. They often include a correction to control for errors.

Power

The probability of correctly rejecting the null hypothesis when it is false. In simple terms, it's the chance of finding a real effect if one exists. The target is usually .80 (80%).

Regression

A statistical technique used to model and predict the relationship between a dependent (outcome) variable and one or more independent (predictor) variables.

Scatterplot

A graph used to visually represent the relationship between two continuous variables, where each dot represents a pair of scores.

Simple Effects

An analysis used to break down a significant interaction. It looks at the effect of one independent variable at each individual level of the other independent variable.

Slope ($b_1$)

In regression, this value represents the change in the outcome variable (Y) for a one-unit increase in the predictor variable (X). It determines the steepness of the regression line.

Sphericity

An assumption for repeated-measures ANOVAs with 3+ levels. It assumes the variances of the differences between all possible pairs of conditions are equal. Tested with Mauchly's Test.

Standard Deviation (SD)

A measure of the average amount of variability or spread in a set of scores. It is the square root of the variance.

Statistical Significance

A result is statistically significant if it is unlikely to have occurred by random chance alone. In psychology, the threshold is typically a p-value less than .05.

Sum of Squares (SS)

A fundamental concept in ANOVA; it's the sum of the squared differences of each score from the mean. It's a measure of total variability.

Systematic Variance

The variability in data that is attributable to the experimenter's manipulation (the independent variable). It's the 'signal' in an ANOVA.

t-Test

A statistical test used to compare the means of exactly two groups.

Type I Error

A "false positive." Rejecting the null hypothesis when it was actually true.

Type II Error

A "false negative." Failing to reject the null hypothesis when it was actually false.

Unsystematic Variance

The variability in data that is due to random, unpredictable factors like individual differences or measurement error. It's the 'noise' in an ANOVA.

Variance

A measure of the spread or dispersion of a set of scores. It's the average of the squared deviations from the mean.

Within-Subjects Design (Repeated Measures)

An experimental design where the same participants take part in all conditions.
