Factorial ANOVA Topics

Two-Way Independent, Two-Way Repeated, and Mixed ANOVA

Two-Way Independent ANOVA

A Two-Way ANOVA is used when you have two independent variables (also called factors). For example, a 2x2 design has two IVs, each with two levels. A 2x3 design has two IVs, one with two levels and one with three. A Two-Way ANOVA allows you to test:

  • Main Effect of IV 1: The overall effect of your first factor, ignoring the levels of the second.
  • Main Effect of IV 2: The overall effect of your second factor, ignoring the levels of the first.
  • Interaction Effect: This is the most interesting part. It tests whether the effect of one independent variable depends on the level of the other independent variable.

Application Corner: Counting the F-Ratios

A Two-Way ANOVA always tests three things, so you will always get three important F-ratios in your output table.

  • One F-ratio for the Main Effect of your first IV.
  • One F-ratio for the Main Effect of your second IV.
  • One F-ratio for the Interaction between them.

The Golden Rule of Interactions: Always interpret the interaction effect first! If it's significant, it qualifies the main effects. This means the main effects might be misleading on their own, because the interaction tells you the full story is more complex.

Step 1: Check the interaction effect. Look at the `drug * therapy` line. The p-value is .125. Since this is greater than .05, the interaction is not significant. This tells us that the effect of the drug is the same regardless of therapy, and vice versa. Because there is no interaction, we can now look at the main effects with confidence.

Step 2: Check the main effect of `drug`. The p-value is `< .001`, which is significant. This means that at least one of the drug conditions is different from another. However, it doesn't tell us where the difference is. We need post-hoc tests for this.

Step 3: Check the main effect of `therapy`. The p-value is `.013`, which is significant. Since there are only two levels (CBT and No Therapy), we don't need post-hoc tests. We can conclude that, overall, CBT resulted in greater mood gain than no therapy.

Final Write-Up

"A 2(therapy) x 3(drug) between-subjects ANOVA was conducted. There was a significant main effect of therapy, F(1, 12) = 8.58, p = .013, ηp2 = .096, indicating that participants who received CBT had a greater mood gain (M = 1.0) than those who did not (M = 0.7). There was also a significant main effect of drug, F(2, 12) = 31.7, p < .001, ηp2 = .713$. Bonferroni post-hoc tests revealed that mood gain was significantly greater with Joyzepam (M = 1.5) than with either Anxifree (M = 0.7, p < .001) or the placebo (M = 0.5, p < .001). The difference between Anxifree and the placebo was not significant (p = .214). The interaction between therapy and drug was not significant, F(2, 12) = 2.49, p = .125, ηp2 = .056$."

Two-Way Repeated-Measures ANOVA

Use this design when you have two independent variables and the same participants take part in all possible conditions.

Application Corner: Decoding the Design

When you see a design described as, for example, a "2x3 repeated-measures design", this tells you everything you need to know:

  • The number of numbers tells you how many IVs there are. Here, "2x3" has two numbers, so there are two IVs.
  • Each number tells you how many levels that IV has. So, one IV has 2 levels (e.g., Sugar: Normal, Extra) and the second IV has 3 levels (e.g., Caffeine: None, Low, High).
  • The total number of conditions is just the numbers multiplied: 2 * 3 = 6 conditions.

A Worked Example: Fizzy Drinks

A company tests how liking ratings (0-10) for a drink are affected by two IVs: Caffeine (None, Low, High) and Sugar (Normal, Extra). The same 5 people taste all 6 versions of the drink. This is a 3x2 repeated-measures design.

As always, we check the interaction first. Remember for repeated-measures, we also need to check the assumption of sphericity.

Step 1: Check the interaction effect. Look at the `Caffeine * Sugar` line. The p-value is .002. Since this is less than .05, the interaction is significant. This is our main finding! It means the effect of caffeine on liking depends on the amount of sugar in the drink. Because the interaction is significant, we should not interpret the main effects directly. The story is in the interaction.

Step 2: Unpack the interaction. To understand the interaction, we need to look at the effects of one IV at each level of the other IV (this is called a simple effects analysis). A good way to do this is to look at the graph and run some follow-up tests.

Interaction Plot: Liking Ratings

Looking at the graph, the lines are not parallel, which confirms our interaction. For the 'Extra Sugar' drink (red line), liking is fairly flat across caffeine levels. But for the 'Normal Sugar' drink (blue line), liking shoots up dramatically in the 'High Caffeine' condition. This is the interaction. Let's confirm with follow-up paired t-tests comparing sugar levels at each caffeine level.

  • At 'High Caffeine', the difference between Normal Sugar (M=7.0) and Extra Sugar (M=4.8) is significant, t(4) = 2.99, p = .040$.
  • At 'No' and 'Low' caffeine levels, the differences are not significant.

Final Write-Up

"A 3(Caffeine: None, Low, High) x 2(Sugar: Normal, Extra) repeated-measures ANOVA was conducted. There was a significant interaction between caffeine and sugar on liking ratings, F(2, 8) = 14.89, p = .002, ηp2 = .788$. Follow-up paired t-tests to investigate this interaction revealed that for the high caffeine drink, liking was significantly higher with normal sugar (M = 7.00) than with extra sugar (M = 4.80), t(4) = 2.99, p = .040$. There were no significant differences between sugar levels for the no caffeine or low caffeine drinks. This indicates that a high level of caffeine increases the popularity of the drink, but only when it has a normal amount of sugar."

Mixed ANOVA

A Mixed ANOVA is your tool of choice when you have a mix of variable types: at least one between-subjects IV (different groups of people) and at least one within-subjects IV (everyone does all levels).

Application Corner: What Makes a Design "Mixed"?

A "mixed" design simply means you have a mix of IV types. To identify one, look for:

  • At least one Between-Subjects IV: Where you compare different groups of people. (Example: Comparing an 'Expert' group to a 'Novice' group).
  • At least one Within-Subjects IV: Where the same people are tested in all conditions. (Example: Testing everyone on a 'Configured' list AND a 'Random' list).

If your design has both types, it's a mixed design.

A Worked Example: Expert Memory

Researchers test memory for London street names. There are two IVs: Expertise (Expert cab drivers vs. Novice students) and List Type (Configured route vs. Random list). Expertise is a between-subjects factor (you're either an expert or a novice), but List Type is a within-subjects factor, as everyone is tested on both list types.

In a mixed ANOVA, JASP gives you two separate output tables for the main analysis: one for the within-subjects effects, and one for the between-subjects effects. Remember the golden rule: interpret the interaction first!

They are fantastic at recalling a configured route (M=14.70) but no better than novices when the list is random (M=6.40). This is the interaction: expertise only provides an advantage when the material is structured in a meaningful way.

Final Write-Up

"A 2 (Group: expert vs. novice) x 2 (Route: configured vs. random) mixed ANOVA was performed. This revealed a significant interaction between group and route type, F(1, 38) = 109.9, p < .001, ηp2 = .743$. Analysis of the simple effects showed that expert taxi drivers recalled significantly more street names when they formed a configured route (M = 14.70) compared to a random list (M = 6.40). In contrast, novices showed no difference in recall between configured (M = 6.45) and random lists (M = 6.50). While the main effects for Group, F(1, 38) = 58.9, p < .001$, and Route, F(1, 38) = 107.3, p < .001$, were also significant, they are qualified by the interaction."
